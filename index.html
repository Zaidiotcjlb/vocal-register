About Vocal Registers

Vocal registers refer to the range of tones produced by the human voice, characterized by their vibratory pattern of the vocal folds. While frequency is a primary indicator, timbre and other factors also contribute to register classification.

Vocal Fry

The lowest register, characterized by a creaky, popping sound. Frequency range: 20-60 Hz. Often used at the very bottom of a singer's range.

Chest Voice

The rich, full sound most people use for speaking. Frequency range: 60-200 Hz. Resonates in the chest cavity and feels vibrations in the chest.

Mixed Voice

A blend of chest and head voice that allows smooth transition between registers. Frequency range: 200-400 Hz. Provides a balanced, powerful sound.

Head Voice

A lighter, more resonant sound that vibrates in the head. Frequency range: 300-600 Hz. Often used for higher pitches in singing.

Falsetto

A breathy, flute-like register used for very high notes. Frequency range: 400-2000 Hz. More common in male singers extending beyond their normal range.

Whistle Register

The highest register with a flute-like, piercing quality. Frequency range: 1000-2500 Hz. Rare and difficult to control for many singers.
